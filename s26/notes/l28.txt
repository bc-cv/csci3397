**Lecture 28: Parameter-Efficient Tuning (PEFT)**

**Total Time: 50 minutes**

**[5 min] 0. Housekeeping**
- **Recap**: Lec. 27 — self-supervised pretraining (MAE, DINO) gives us powerful foundation models.
- **The Problem**: These models have hundreds of millions (or billions) of parameters. How do we adapt them to new tasks without retraining everything?
- **Reading**: LoRA paper (Hu et al., arXiv:2106.09685); HuggingFace PEFT tutorial.

**[10 min] 1. The Adaptation Problem**
- **Full Fine-Tuning**: Update all parameters on downstream data.
  - Pros: Maximum expressivity, well-understood.
  - Cons: Expensive (GPU memory, storage); one copy of the model per task; risk of catastrophic forgetting.
- **Linear Probing**: Freeze the backbone, train only a classification head.
  - Pros: Cheap, fast.
  - Cons: Limited capacity; cannot adapt internal representations.
- **The Gap**: We want something between these two extremes — update a small fraction of parameters while achieving near-full-fine-tuning performance.

**[10 min] 2. Adapter Modules**
- **Key Idea**: Insert small trainable bottleneck layers into a frozen pretrained network.
- **Architecture**: Input → down-projection (d → r) → nonlinearity → up-projection (r → d) → residual connection.
  - r << d, so very few new parameters (typically <5% of the original model).
- **Where to Insert**: After attention and/or FFN sub-layers in each transformer block.
- **Training**: Only adapter parameters are updated; backbone stays frozen.
- **Advantages**: Task-specific adapters are lightweight; multiple tasks share the same backbone.

**[10 min] 3. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)**
- **Key Idea**: Instead of adding new layers, reparameterize existing weight updates as low-rank matrices.
- **Formulation**: W' = W + BA, where W is frozen (d × d), B is (d × r), A is (r × d), and r << d.
  - Only B and A are trained; W is never modified.
  - At inference: merge BA into W — zero additional latency.
- **Why Low-Rank Works**: Weight updates during fine-tuning empirically have low intrinsic rank.
- **Hyperparameters**: Rank r (typical: 4–64), which layers to adapt (query/value projections work well).
- **Comparison to Adapters**: No added inference cost; even fewer parameters; easy to swap task-specific LoRA weights.

**[10 min] 4. Biomedical Application: Adapting FMs to New Organs/Stains**
- **Scenario**: A ViT pretrained on ImageNet or large pathology datasets (e.g., via DINO). New task: classify rare tissue stains or segment a new organ.
- **Why PEFT Fits**:
  - Limited labeled biomedical data — full fine-tuning overfits.
  - Hospital compute budgets are small — cannot retrain billion-parameter models.
  - Multiple downstream tasks (different organs, stains, modalities) share one frozen backbone.
- **Practical Workflow** (HuggingFace PEFT):
  1. Load pretrained model (e.g., from timm or HuggingFace Hub).
  2. Attach LoRA adapters with a few lines of code.
  3. Train on domain-specific data; save only the adapter weights (~MBs vs. GBs).
  4. Swap adapters at inference for different tasks.
- **Results**: LoRA often matches or approaches full fine-tuning on biomedical benchmarks with <1% of trainable parameters.

**[5 min] 5. Broader PEFT Landscape & Wrap-Up**
- **Other Methods**: Prefix tuning (learnable prompt tokens), BitFit (bias-only tuning), (IA)³ (learned rescaling).
- **How to Choose**: LoRA is the current default; adapters if you need modularity; prefix tuning for generative tasks.
- **Looking Ahead**: Lec. 29 — Promptable Segmentation (SAM, MedSAM): a different kind of adaptation where the user provides spatial prompts at inference time.
